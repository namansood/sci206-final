Types of Motion

Translational Motion

This is the kind of motion where all parts of an object move the same distance with respect to each other, that is, the entire object moves as a single entity.

Rotational Motion

This is the kind of motion where an object moves about an axis and different parts of it move different distances in a given time – the parts closer to the axis move lesser than the parts that are further away.

Earth animation adapted from Deyvid Bustos's work.

Periodic Motion

This is the kind of motion where the motion performed by an object is repeated after equal intervals of time. One complete repetition of the motion is called a cycle.

Nonperiodic Motion

This is the kind of motion where the motion performed by an object is not repeated after regular intervals of time. However, the motion may repeat after irregular intervals of time, or it may not repeat at all.

First Law

An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion, until and unless a force is applied to it.

Until we apply a force on the balls, they remain at rest. When the balls are moving, they only come to a stop when a resisting force is applied.

Second Law

The force applied on an object is directly proportional to its acceleration.

If we apply “more force” by pushing a ball multiple times in the same direction, the ball is accelerated more and hence gains more velocity.

Third Law

For every action (force) in nature, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

When the two balls collide, one ball applies a force on the second, and at the same time experiences an equal force in the opposite direction from the second ball.

Gravitational Force

Mass and Weight

The gravitational force is the force that attracts any two objects with mass.

The difference between weight and mass is that mass remains constant for an object throughout the universe, whereas weight can change depending on the the gravitational force.

Since weighing machines measure weight and not mass, a weighing machine designed for the Earth would give different readings on another celestial body, like Mars or the Moon – as you can see in the calculator on the right!

Other Important Forces

Normal Force

According to Newton's third law, surfaces in contact with an object exert a force on the object that is equal and opposite to the force the object is exerting on them. This is called the normal force.

Tension

This is the force that is transmitted though a rope or string that has forces acting on it at both ends. Tension in a string is always equal across the length of the string.

Friction

The frictional force is the force generated by two objects in contact with each other. This force is primarily of three types: static, kinetic and sliding. Static friction is the highest in magnitude, followed by kinetic, and finally sliding.
